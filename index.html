Responsive Redesign

Improving the user interface and user experience for a movie review app

Overview

In this project, I created sketches, lo-fi, and hi-fi designs for the LetterBoxd app. Then, I developed this redesign with HTML and CSS.

Part 1: Identifying Usability Problems

Picking a Webpage

The website that I'm redesigning is the “Reviews Page” from Letterboxd, a social media platform where users rate and comment on movies and TV shows for their friends to see. See full website here!

I chose this website, because I feel it's outdated and confusing to navigate, as there’s a lot of visual elements that are catching my attention and not highlighting the most important elements when I’m reading a review section.

Finding Problems

Next, I analyzed the problems with the application's interface by creating an infographic with annotations of the elements and writing a list about its Learnability, Memorability, and Efficiency.

Here are the main issues with the interface.

Learnability:

  • Text for different links (rather than icons is helpful), but feels too cluttered and even overwhelming for new users
  • Hover states only show two icons, but most users want it to put it in a list, so it’s hard for users to know how to add to a list since there’s no quick icon to go to
  • Friend (or other) reviews aren’t shown immediately unless users click on each friend icon

Memorability:

  • Workflow is similar to DVD stands “RedBox” and “BlockBuster,” as shape and grid display is familiar
  • Review ratings and comments is similar to most movie review and social media comment sections with the 5 star rating system
  • “Reviewed,” “Like,” and “Watchlist” icons feel that they’re all similar, and users can hesitate on what to categorize the movie, since they’re all the same size

Efficiency:

  • Bigger buttons are displayed for writing reviews and editing their star ratings
  • Logic of tags being at the bottom and the rating and commentary being first is more efficient, as most important content is being showed first from top to bottom
  • Adding comments is confusing, as there are multiple “steps” to actually put and type a comment (click on review, click on small icon, enter comment section as a whole new page, click empty text field, enter comment)

Accessibility

Finally, I first used "WebAIM WAVE" to detect possible accessibility problems. However, it wasn't able to generate a report, so I reverted to using "Lighthouse" instead. The score came back with a score of 87/100.

Main Issues:

  • Background and foreground colors do not have a sufficient contrast ratio
  • Links do not have a discernible name
  • Image elements have [alt] attributes that are redundant text

Here is the Lighthouse Report for the old design.

Based on the findings, I mostly agree with the accessibility problems raised from the report. A lot of the icons (search icon, calendar icons, etc.) don’t have descriptive text on hover or as an “aria-label.” Likewise, I also found some of the issues about color contrast accessibility to be interesting, because from my point of view, the contrast looks legible; however, it’s important to note for users who have visual limitations are affected.
